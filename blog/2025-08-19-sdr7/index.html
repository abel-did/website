      Phase-Locked Loop

      August 16, 2025·Reading time : 7 minutes

      Phase-locked loops, known by the acronym PLL (Phase Locked Loop), constitute the invisible backbone of our modern electronic world. From smartphones to satellites, including the most sophisticated metrology equipment, these ingenious circuits silently orchestrate the generation and synchronization of millions of signals. Understanding their operation means grasping one of the fundamental mechanisms that allow our communication systems to function with remarkable precision.

      Objective of the phase-locked loop

      A PLL pursues an apparently simple but technically ambitious objective: to generate an output signal whose phase remains perfectly synchronized with that of a reference signal. This phase synchronization naturally implies frequency equality, transforming the PLL into a true programmable frequency synthesizer.

      The principle relies on a negative feedback loop that continuously compares the phase of the generated signal with that of a reference. Any drift is immediately detected and corrected, thus maintaining precise locking. This locking capability offers three major advantages. First, it allows multiplying or dividing a reference frequency with extreme precision, thus creating signals at precise frequencies different from the reference. Second, it effectively filters phase noise, producing signals of remarkable spectral purity. Finally, it ensures long-term stability directly inherited from the reference, generally a high-quality quartz oscillator.

      In the context of radiocommunications, PLLs fulfill critical functions. They generate the carriers necessary for transmitting modulated signals and create the local oscillators essential for frequency conversion stages in reception. They also participate in the demodulation of FM and FSK signals, exploiting the VCO control voltage as a demodulated signal. In digital systems, they recover the clock embedded in transmitted data, allowing perfect synchronization between transmitter and receiver.

      Stable frequency signal requirements in a receiver

      A modern radio receiver, whether it’s a classic superheterodyne or based on an SDR architecture, requires a multitude of reference signals of impeccable stability. This requirement stems directly from the physical and technical constraints imposed by radioelectric signal processing.

      Take the example of a superheterodyne receiver tuned to 100 MHz with a channel bandwidth of 25 kHz. The local oscillator must maintain its frequency with precision better than ±1 kHz to ensure that the signal remains centered in the intermediate frequency filter. This represents a relative stability of 10 ppm (parts per million), already difficult to achieve with a simple LC oscillator. For modern digital communications, where channels may be spaced by only a few kilohertz, requirements become even stricter, requiring stabilities of the order of 1 ppm or better.

      Frequency drift of free-running oscillators constitutes a major problem. A classic LC oscillator can drift by several kilohertz over a temperature range of 50°C, making stable reception of a narrow-band signal impossible. Supply voltage variations, component aging, and mechanical vibrations further aggravate this instability. Even quartz oscillators, though renowned for their stability, require sophisticated temperature compensation to achieve the required performance.

      This is where the PLL reveals all its power. By locking a variable oscillator (VCO) to a stable reference, it combines the best of both worlds: the frequency agility of the VCO and the long-term stability of the reference. A modern receiver can thus use a single high-quality quartz reference to generate, via several PLLs, all the signals necessary for its operation: local oscillators for different conversion stages, sampling clock for analog-to-digital converters, processing frequencies for digital signal processors.

      Unity feedback phase-locked loop

      The unity feedback PLL represents the most elementary form of phase-locked loop, where the output signal is directly fed back to the phase detector without modification. This architecture, although simple, perfectly illustrates the fundamental mechanisms of phase locking.

      At the heart of this loop, three essential elements interact permanently. The phase detector compares the instantaneous phases of the reference signal and output signal, producing an error voltage proportional to their difference. This voltage then passes through a loop filter, generally a low-pass filter, which determines the system’s response dynamics. The filter eliminates unwanted high-frequency components while defining the loop bandwidth. Finally, the voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO) generates the output signal whose frequency varies linearly with the control voltage from the filter.

      The locking process establishes itself progressively. Initially, if the frequencies differ, the phase detector produces a beat at the difference frequency. This low-frequency component, after filtering, modulates the VCO which progressively approaches the reference frequency. When the gap becomes sufficiently small, the loop “locks” and maintains stable locking. The VCO phase then faithfully follows that of the reference, with a constant offset determined by the loop characteristics.

      The design of the loop filter is of paramount importance. A first-order filter, simple parallel capacitance, offers stable response but a relatively long acquisition time. Second-order filters, integrating resistances and capacitances, allow optimization of the compromise between acquisition speed and stability. The filter bandwidth directly defines the PLL’s capture range, i.e., the maximum initial frequency deviation allowing locking, as well as the loop’s ability to follow rapid variations in the reference.

      Non-unity feedback phase-locked loop

      The non-unity feedback architecture transforms the PLL into a true programmable frequency synthesizer. By inserting a frequency divider in the feedback loop, the VCO is forced to oscillate at an exact multiple of the reference frequency, thus opening the way to generating thousands of different frequencies from a single reference.

      The principle relies on an elegant trick: the phase detector compares the reference not directly to the VCO signal, but to this signal divided by a programmable factor N. At equilibrium, the two compared signals must have the same frequency. If the reference oscillates at fref, the VCO must therefore oscillate at N × fref so that, after division, the frequencies are identical at the comparator level. By simply modifying the value of N, the output frequency is instantly changed in steps of fref.

      This architecture offers remarkable flexibility for modern radiocommunication systems. A transceiver covering the 88-108 MHz band with channels spaced 100 kHz apart can use a 100 kHz reference and vary N from 880 to 1080. The frequency resolution equals the reference frequency, allowing any channel to be reached. However, this resolution imposes a compromise: a low-frequency reference offers fine resolution but slows the loop response, while a high reference accelerates the loop but limits resolution.

      Modern fractional PLLs circumvent this limitation by using dividers capable of non-integer ratios. Through sophisticated sigma-delta modulation techniques, they can generate frequencies with resolution much finer than the reference frequency, while maintaining high loop bandwidth. These architectures allow ultra-fast switching times, essential for frequency-hopping systems or cognitive radios that must rapidly explore the spectrum.

      Increasing integration has given birth to remarkable monolithic PLL circuits. A modern synthesizer integrates on a few square millimeters of silicon all loop elements, including multi-band VCOs covering several gigahertz, high-speed programmable dividers, digital phase detectors, and even adaptive loop filters.

      Conclusion

      Phase-locked loops embody the elegance of electronic engineering: a simple principle - phase feedback - which, properly implemented, solves complex problems of signal generation and synchronization. From the discrete analog PLL of the 1930s to today’s integrated fractional synthesizers, this technology has continuously evolved to meet the ever-increasing demands of communication systems.

      In the context of software-defined radio that we explore in this series of articles, PLLs play a fundamental role by providing the temporal and frequency references essential for the proper functioning of converters and digital processing algorithms. Understanding their operation allows better appreciation of the performance and limitations of modern SDR systems, where the quality of frequency synthesis directly conditions the receiver’s sensitivity, selectivity, and dynamic range.
