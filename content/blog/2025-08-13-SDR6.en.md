Software-defined radio (SDR) represents a revolution in the field of radio communications by replacing traditional analog circuits with digital algorithms executed on processors, DSPs or FPGAs. When a radio signal is captured by an antenna, it is digitized by an analog-to-digital converter (ADC), producing a stream of complex samples (I/Q) that enters a digital processing chain. This chain transforms raw data into exploitable information, such as audio for an FM station, data for a Wi-Fi network, or navigation signals for GPS. The power of SDR lies in its flexibility: the same hardware can be reconfigured by software to process different protocols or modulations, from FM broadcasting at 98 MHz to LTE signal reception, without requiring hardware modifications. This flexibility relies on a carefully orchestrated sequence of digital operations: filtering to eliminate interference, sampling rate adjustment to optimize calculations, frequency translation to center the signal, channel extraction to isolate the useful band, equalization to correct distortions, and management of latency and throughput constraints for real-time processing.

#### Digital filtering: FIR and IIR

Once digitized by the ADC, the raw signal often contains interference from frequencies outside the band of interest, such as adjacent signals or noise. Digital filtering constitutes the first major step to clean this signal and limit its bandwidth to that necessary for subsequent processing. For example, in an SDR capturing a wide spectrum at 2 megasamples per second (Msps), a filter can isolate a 200 kHz FM channel. Two types of filters dominate in this context: finite impulse response (FIR) and infinite impulse response (IIR) filters. FIR filters calculate their output by convolution, where each output sample is a linear combination of input samples weighted by predefined coefficients, according to the equation: $$ y[n] = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} h[k]x[n-k] $$

This method guarantees absolute stability, as there is no feedback, and linear phase, essential for preserving signal shape in complex modulations like QAM. For example, a 64-coefficient FIR filter can be designed to isolate a 1.536 MHz DAB+ channel with high precision. In contrast, IIR filters integrate feedback, where the output depends on both current inputs and previous outputs, following an equation of the form: $$ y[n] = \sum_{k=0}^{N} b[k]x[n-k] - \sum_{k=1}^{M} a[k]y[n-k]$$

They offer high selectivity with fewer coefficients, which reduces computational load, but their nonlinear phase and risk of instability make them less suitable for critical applications. In an SDR, FIR filters are often preferred for precise reception, such as in digital broadcasting systems, while IIR are used for fast pre-filters or applications where phase is less critical.

#### Decimation and interpolation

After initial filtering, it is often necessary to adjust the signal's sampling frequency to reduce computational load or prepare specific processing. Decimation consists of decreasing this frequency, for example going from 2 Msps to 200 kilosamples per second (ksps) to process an FM channel. Simple sample suppression would cause aliasing, where out-of-band frequencies fold into the useful spectrum, degrading information. To avoid this, a low-pass filter is applied before decimation, with a cutoff frequency lower than half the new sampling frequency, in accordance with the Nyquist-Shannon theorem. Conversely, interpolation increases the sampling rate by inserting calculated samples, generally zeros, followed by low-pass filtering to smooth the signal. For example, an SDR capturing a wide spectrum at 20 Msps can decimate to 200 ksps to isolate a 20 MHz Wi-Fi channel, thus reducing computation requirements for subsequent steps. These techniques are essential for adapting the data flow to processing algorithm constraints, such as in wideband receivers where multiple channels must be efficiently isolated. They also allow preparing the signal for specific modulations or transmissions, optimizing hardware resource utilization.

#### Frequency translation (digital mixing)

Frequency translation, or digital mixing, plays a crucial role by moving the signal in the spectrum to center it around 0 Hz, in baseband, to simplify subsequent processing. In an analog radio, a mixer combines the signal with a sinusoid generated by a local oscillator. Digitally, this operation is performed by multiplying samples by a complex sinusoid, produced by a numerical oscillator (NCO), according to the equation: $$ x'[n] = x[n] \cdot e^{-j2\pi f_0 n / f_s} $$, where \( f_0 \) is the translation frequency and \( f_s \) the sampling frequency. For example, an FM signal initially centered at 1 MHz after a first analog mix can be brought back to 0 Hz to facilitate audio extraction. This step is essential for isolating a specific channel in a wide spectrum, such as an LTE signal in a 20 MHz band, and reduces demodulation algorithm complexity. NCO precision is critical to avoid frequency errors.

#### Channel extraction

Once the signal is centered in baseband, channel extraction allows isolating the bandwidth of interest, such as a 200 kHz FM channel or a 1.536 MHz DAB+ channel, from the digitized spectrum. This step combines selective filtering, often performed with an FIR filter for its precision, and decimation to adapt the sampling rate to the channel bandwidth. For example, an SDR receiver capturing a wide spectrum at 10 Msps can extract a DAB+ channel by applying a low-pass filter followed by decimation to 2 Msps, thus reducing the amount of data to process. This operation ensures that only relevant samples are transmitted to subsequent steps, optimizing system efficiency. In applications such as Wi-Fi signal reception or digital television, channel extraction is a key step for isolating a specific signal among several simultaneously captured channels.

#### Equalization

The radio propagation channel often introduces distortions, such as selective attenuation of certain frequencies or interference due to echoes, which degrade signal quality. Equalization compensates for these effects to restore signal integrity before demodulation. Fixed equalizers rely on prior channel estimation, often obtained via pilot signals, as in OFDM systems used for Wi-Fi or 4G. Adaptive equalizers, on the other hand, adjust their coefficients in real time using algorithms like LMS (Least Mean Squares), particularly useful in changing environments, such as Wi-Fi reception in urban environments with multiple reflections. For example, an adaptive equalizer can correct inter-symbol interference in a QAM signal, ensuring a clear constellation for reliable demodulation. This step is crucial for complex modulations where distortions can compromise data recovery.

#### Latency and processing throughput

The performance of a digital processing chain in an SDR depends on two critical constraints: latency and processing throughput. Latency represents the delay between signal input into the chain and its processed output, influenced by factors such as filter length or processing block size. For example, a 64-coefficient FIR filter at 2 Msps introduces a delay of about 32 microseconds, which can be problematic in real-time applications like VoIP telephony. Processing throughput, meanwhile, measures the amount of data processed per second. A 10 Msps signal requires processing 10 million complex samples per second, which may necessitate specialized processors like DSPs or FPGAs to meet computational requirements. For example, a 128-coefficient FIR filter on a 2 Msps signal requires about 256 million operations per second (MFLOPS), a load that a modern PC can handle, but which exceeds the capabilities of a basic microcontroller. These constraints are essential for guaranteeing real-time processing, particularly in applications such as broadcasting or cellular communications.

#### Conclusion

The digital processing chain in a software-defined radio transforms a raw digitized signal into exploitable information through a logical and well-defined sequence: filtering eliminates interference, decimation and interpolation optimize sampling rate, frequency translation centers the signal in baseband, channel extraction isolates the useful band, equalization corrects distortions, and latency and throughput management guarantees real-time feasibility. Each step plays a precise role, relying on sophisticated algorithms to offer the flexibility that characterizes SDR. Whether receiving an FM station, decoding a Wi-Fi signal or experimenting with amateur radio protocols, these operations form the foundation of a high-performance SDR.
