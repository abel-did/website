The Internet of Things (IoT) imposes unprecedented cryptographic constraints, requiring devices with limited resources to maintain secure communications for decades. These constraints redefine the application of fundamental cryptographic principles and demand a deep understanding of trade-offs between security, performance, and energy efficiency. In an ecosystem where connected objects—from sensors to household devices—proliferate, security must adapt to environments with low computational power, memory, and energy. This article explores these challenges through a structured outline, proposing practical approaches to secure IoT while preserving its efficiency.

#### Kerckhoffs’ Principle in IoT

Kerckhoffs’ principle states that a cryptographic system must remain secure even if everything except the key is publicly known. In IoT, this principle is critical due to the constrained nature of devices. Security through obscurity is ineffective against reverse engineering or leaks. Therefore, standardized algorithms, such as those from NIST, are prioritized, supported by robust key management. For example, Blurry Box Encryption uses hardware anchors to protect confidentiality while adhering to this principle. Trade-offs involve simplified algorithms to save energy while ensuring long-term security against attacks.

#### Security Levels Tailored to IoT Constraints

Cryptographic algorithms are distinguished by several parameters, with the most critical being their security level, expressed in bits (e.g., 64 bits, 80 bits, or 256 bits). This parameter provides a practical measure for comparing the robustness of algorithms against attacks. It represents the effort an attacker must expend to compromise the protection offered by the algorithm, whether by decrypting a message, forging a signature, or bypassing authentication.

In the IoT context, where devices have limited resources (computational power, memory, energy), choosing the appropriate security level is particularly critical. A security level of *n* bits means an attacker must explore a search space of *2^n* possibilities to find the correct solution, such as a decryption key. For example, an algorithm with a 256-bit security level requires an attacker to test up to *2^256* combinations in the worst case—a computational effort considered practically infeasible with current technology.
However, in IoT, a high security level like 256 bits may be excessive for constrained devices, as it increases energy consumption and computational demands. Thus, algorithms like AES-128 or SPECK, offering a 128-bit security level, are often preferred to balance robustness and efficiency. Additionally, approaches like OSCORE adapt security to the context, reducing computations while maintaining adequate protection for IoT sensors or gateways. The goal is to ensure long-term security over years while respecting the energy and hardware constraints of connected devices.

#### Symmetric Encryption for Constrained Devices

The origins of symmetric cryptography trace back to antiquity, notably to Julius Caesar, the famous Roman emperor, who reportedly used a letter substitution cipher known today as the Caesar cipher. This simple system involved shifting each letter in a message by a fixed number of positions in the alphabet to make the text incomprehensible to those unaware of the key, i.e., the shift used. Though rudimentary, this method illustrates the fundamental principle of symmetric cryptography, which has remained unchanged for centuries.
In symmetric cryptography, a plaintext message is transformed into a ciphertext using an encryption algorithm, often denoted as Encrypt(), and a secret cryptographic key. This key, shared only between the sender and recipient, is essential for ensuring message confidentiality. The decryption process, performed via the Decrypt() algorithm, uses the same key to convert the ciphertext back to the original message. The use of a single key for both operations explains the term "symmetric."

![Symmetric Cryptography](/images/2025-09-14-embsec2/en/FIG1.png "Principle of Symmetric Encryption")

Over time, symmetric cryptography has evolved from manual techniques like the Caesar cipher to complex algorithms such as DES (Data Encryption Standard) in the 1970s or AES (Advanced Encryption Standard), widely used today. These modern algorithms leverage longer keys and sophisticated mathematical transformations to resist computational attacks, including brute-force attempts.

A major advantage of symmetric cryptography is its speed, making it ideal for encrypting large amounts of data. However, it presents a critical challenge: secure key management. Since the same key is used for both encryption and decryption, it must be securely transmitted between parties, often through a separate or protected channel, to prevent interception by an attacker. This is why symmetric cryptography is often combined with asymmetric cryptography techniques, such as RSA, to securely exchange keys before using a symmetric algorithm for data encryption.

#### Hash Functions for IoT Authentication

A hash function is a mathematical operation that transforms data, such as text or a file, into a fixed-size digital fingerprint called a hash. It is fast, consistently produces the same output for the same input, and changes entirely if the input is slightly modified. Hash functions are used to secure passwords, verify file integrity, or in technologies like blockchain. They are designed to be difficult to reverse and to prevent different inputs from producing the same hash. Algorithms like SHA-256 are common, though some, like MD5, are considered less secure today.

#### Asymmetric Cryptography Adapted for IoT

Asymmetric cryptography is an encryption method that relies on two keys: a public key, freely shared, and a private key, kept secret. The public key is used to encrypt a message or verify a signature, while the private key is used to decrypt or sign. The two keys are mathematically linked, but deducing the private key from the public key is nearly impossible. This approach secures communications, such as in emails or HTTPS, authenticates messages via digital signatures, or facilitates secure key exchanges in systems like blockchain. Algorithms like RSA or ECC are common, but this method is slower than symmetric cryptography.

These approaches enable devices to operate securely over the long term, fostering the development of the IoT ecosystem.
