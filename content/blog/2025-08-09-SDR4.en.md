Software-defined radio is built on a simple principle: replacing most analog RF and IF processing stages with digital operations. The transition point between these two worlds is signal digitization. This is the precise moment when an analog signal, captured by the antenna and shaped by the RF front-end, becomes a sequence of samples that can be processed by algorithms. The performance of an SDR is therefore directly linked to the quality of this analog-to-digital conversion.

#### Digitization: Recap and Deep Dive

Analog-to-digital conversion first relies on sampling, i.e., measuring the signal’s amplitude at regular intervals defined by a sampling frequency fefe​.

The Nyquist–Shannon theorem requires that fefe​ be at least twice the maximum frequency contained in the signal; otherwise, irreversible spectral folding known as aliasing will corrupt the information.

In a radio context, it is sometimes possible to circumvent the apparent need for ultra-high sampling rates by using undersampling or bandpass sampling. This technique targets a narrow RF band—even at high frequency—and chooses a sampling frequency that intentionally folds this band into the Nyquist zone, provided the anti-alias filtering is perfectly controlled.

Once the sampling instants are defined, the ADC performs quantization, assigning a digital value encoded over NN bits to each measurement. The resolution obtained determines the theoretical signal-to-noise ratio due to quantization, given by:

$$ SNR_{quant} = 6.02N+1.76 dB $$

Each additional bit doubles the precision and increases the SNR by about 6 dB, but also generates a larger data volume. The raw data rate is equal to the product of fefe​ by NN.

For example, a stream of 250 mega-samples per second at 14 bits already represents 3.5 gigabits per second, requiring the downstream FPGA or DSP to have sufficient internal bandwidth to process the data in real time.

#### Acquisition Architectures in SDR

The choice of acquisition architecture determines the complexity of the front-end and the constraints placed on the ADC.

The heterodyne approach remains very common: the RF signal is first shifted to a lower intermediate frequency using a mixer, then filtered before digitization. This method reduces the required sampling frequency and allows the use of less expensive ADCs that are less sensitive to clock jitter. However, it requires multiple analog stages, which come with their own drawbacks: intermodulation distortion, local oscillator phase noise, thermal drift, and the need for regular calibration.

At the opposite end, direct sampling feeds the ADC directly with the RF band. This approach eliminates the intermediate frequency and greatly simplifies the analog chain, while offering a very wide instantaneous bandwidth. However, it demands extremely fast converters—often operating at several gigasamples per second—whose cost, power consumption, and sensitivity to jitter are significantly higher.

Between these two extremes, bandpass sampling occupies a special place. It relies on the deliberate spectral folding of an RF band into the Nyquist zone by precisely choosing the sampling frequency. This makes it possible to process high-frequency signals using a slower ADC, but requires very selective input filtering to prevent undesirable overlap from other bands.

#### Critical Parameters

Merely specifying the sampling frequency and number of bits is not enough to describe the actual performance of an SDR chain. Other parameters directly influence the quality of the digitized signal.

ENOB (Effective Number of Bits) expresses the effective resolution while accounting for the noise and distortion generated by the converter. It is always lower than the theoretical resolution and tends to decrease as the input frequency increases.

SFDR (Spurious-Free Dynamic Range) measures the system’s ability to detect a weak signal in the presence of strong interfering signals. In crowded RF environments, a high SFDR is crucial to prevent intermodulation products from masking signals of interest.

The sampling clock jitter also plays a major role, especially at high frequencies. For example, an RMS jitter of 100 femtoseconds limits the SNR to about 70 dB for a 100 MHz signal—and considerably less for a signal in the gigahertz range.

Finally, total latency—the delay between RF capture and the availability of processed samples—can become critical in applications such as radar, real-time bidirectional communications, or adaptive control systems.

The overall dynamic range, which combines ADC resolution, SFDR, and automatic gain control, determines the system’s ability to handle signals of widely varying amplitudes without distortion or saturation.
