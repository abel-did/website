Software-defined radio is based on a simple principle: replace most analog RF and IF processing with digital operations. The tipping point between these two worlds is signal digitization. It is at this precise moment that an analog signal, captured by the antenna and shaped by the RF front-end, becomes a series of samples exploitable by algorithms. The performance of an SDR is therefore directly linked to the quality of this analog-to-digital conversion.

#### Digitization: reminders and deeper insights

Analog-to-digital conversion first relies on sampling, that is, measuring the signal amplitude at regular intervals defined by a sampling frequency \( f_e \).

The Nyquist–Shannon theorem requires that \( f_e \) be at least twice the maximum frequency contained in the signal, failing which irreversible spectral aliasing will corrupt the information. In a radio context, it is sometimes possible to circumvent the apparent constraint of ultra-fast sampling by exploiting undersampling, or *bandpass sampling*. This technique consists of targeting a narrow RF band, even if located at high frequency, and choosing a sampling frequency that deliberately folds this band into the Nyquist zone, provided that anti-aliasing filtering is perfectly controlled.

Once the sampling instants are defined, the analog-to-digital converter proceeds to quantization, that is, assigning a digital value coded on a certain number of bits \( N \) to each measurement. The resolution thus obtained determines the theoretical signal-to-noise ratio related to quantization, given by the relation

\[ SNR_{quant} = 6.02N + 1.76 \, \text{dB} \]

Each additional bit doubles the precision and increases the SNR by about 6 dB, but also generates a larger volume of data. Indeed, the raw data rate to process equals the product of \( f_e \) by \( N \). For example, a stream of 250 mega-samples per second on 14 bits already represents 3.5 gigabits per second, which requires the downstream FPGA or DSP to have sufficient internal bandwidth to process this data in real time.

#### Acquisition architectures in SDR

The choice of acquisition architecture determines the front-end complexity and constraints imposed on the ADC. The heterodyne approach remains very widespread: the RF signal is first translated to a lower intermediate frequency using a mixer, then filtered before being digitized. This method allows reducing the required sampling frequency and using less expensive ADCs that are less sensitive to clock jitter. However, it requires multiplying analog stages, with associated drawbacks: intermodulation distortion, local oscillator phase noise, thermal drift and the need for regular calibrations.

At the opposite end, direct digitization, or *direct sampling*, consists of attacking the ADC directly in RF band. This solution eliminates the intermediate frequency and considerably simplifies the analog chain, while offering a very wide instantaneous band. However, it requires very fast converters, often at several giga-samples per second, whose cost, consumption and jitter sensitivity are much higher.

Between these two approaches, undersampling, or *bandpass sampling*, occupies a special place. It relies on deliberate spectral folding of an RF band into the Nyquist zone through precise choice of sampling frequency. This allows processing high-frequency signals with a slower ADC, but requires very selective input filtering to avoid unwanted superposition of other bands.

#### Critical parameters

Simply mentioning sampling frequency and number of bits is not sufficient to describe the real performance of an SDR chain. Other parameters directly influence the quality of the digitized signal. The ENOB, or *Effective Number of Bits*, expresses the effective resolution taking into account noise and distortion generated by the converter. It is always lower than the theoretical resolution and tends to decrease when the input frequency increases. The SFDR, or *Spurious-Free Dynamic Range*, measures the system's ability to distinguish a weak signal in the presence of strong parasitic signals. In crowded RF environments, a high SFDR is crucial to prevent intermodulation products from masking signals of interest.

The sampling clock jitter also plays a major role, especially at high frequencies. An RMS jitter of 100 femtoseconds limits for example the SNR to about 70 dB for a 100 MHz signal, and to much less for a gigahertz signal. Finally, the total latency, that is, the delay between RF capture and availability of processed samples, can become critical in applications such as radar, real-time bidirectional communications or adaptive control systems. The overall dynamic range, which combines ADC resolution, SFDR and automatic gain management, determines the system's ability to simultaneously process signals of very different levels without distortion or saturation.

#### Conclusion

Signal digitization in SDR systems is a key process that relies on a balance between sampling frequency, resolution and management of constraints imposed by the RF environment. The Nyquist–Shannon theorem and spectral aliasing management define the sampling basics, while architectures like heterodyne, direct digitization or undersampling offer complementary approaches adapted to different needs. Each architecture presents advantages and challenges: heterodyne favors ADC simplicity at the cost of a complex analog chain, direct digitization maximizes flexibility at the cost of expensive converters, and undersampling optimizes resources provided precise filtering. Critical parameters, such as ENOB, SFDR, jitter and latency, play a determining role in overall performance, particularly in demanding applications like radar or modern communications. With the evolution of technologies, SDR systems continue to integrate increasingly powerful ADCs and advanced algorithms to meet the growing requirements of bandwidth, precision and real-time processing.
