By addressing notions such as baseband, modulations and channel capacity, we will lay the foundations necessary to understand the technical challenges that SDRs address.

#### Baseband

A **baseband** signal refers to an original signal, as it is produced by the source, before any transformation for transmission. For example, a digital audio file is a baseband signal: its spectrum is centered around zero frequency and extends to a maximum frequency related to its content (a few KHz for voice).

#### Modulations

To transmit this signal over a physical medium (cable, fiber, radio waves), it is often necessary to **modulate** the signal. **Modulation** consists of "carrying" the information on a higher carrier frequency in order to:
* adapt the signal to the transmission channel,
* allow simultaneous transmission of multiple signals (multiplexing),
* reduce interference in certain media.

There are several types of modulations:

- **Amplitude Modulation (AM)**: the amplitude of the carrier varies according to the signal to be transmitted.
- **Frequency Modulation (FM)**: the frequency of the carrier varies according to the signal.
- **Phase Modulation (PM)**: the phase of the carrier changes to encode the information.

#### Shannon and channel capacity

In 1948, Claude Edwood Shannon modeled for the first time a communication channel scheme in his article entitled: **A Mathematical Theory of Communication**. All stakeholders in signal transmission are characterized from the information source to its destination.

![landscape](/images/2025-08-09-sdr2/en/canal_com.png "Communication channel diagram")

The scientist is a pioneer of information theory. He established a fundamental theorem that defines the **capacity of a channel**.

The formula that models this phenomenon is as follows:

$$ C = BW \log_{2}{(1+\frac{S}{N})} $$

* C: Channel capacity (bit/s)
* BW: Bandwidth (Hz)
* S/N: Signal-to-noise ratio (unitless, on linear scale)

Shannon's formula allows calculating the maximum capacity of a channel, that is, the amount of information it can transmit per second. The capacity depends on two important factors:

- bandwidth
- signal-to-noise ratio

In practice, this formula helps us understand how to maximize information transmission in systems such as wireless networks.

Let's take an example to manipulate Shannon's formula.

**Example** - Theoretical capacity vs real throughput (Wi-Fi case)

Let's imagine a **Wi-Fi** network:

* Available bandwidth: B = 20 MHz
* Measured signal-to-noise ratio: S/N = 25 dB

1) **S/N ratio conversion:**

$$ \frac{S}{N} = 10^{\frac{25}{10}} = 10^{2.5} \approx 316.23 $$

2) **Shannon's formula:**

$$ C = BW \log_{2}{(1+\frac{S}{N})} $$
$$ C = 20 \cdot 10^{6} \log_{2}{(1+316.23)} $$
$$ C \approx  20 \cdot 10^{6} \cdot 8.31 \approx 166.2 Mbit/s $$

The theoretical capacity is approximately equal to 166 Mb/s. While the real throughput is often between 80 and 120 Mb/s. This is due to losses, interference, management protocols and collisions. Thus, this shows that in practice, the obtained throughput is always lower than the maximum capacity of a channel.

#### Noise

Noise is any unwanted disturbance that corrupts a radio signal. It can come from natural sources (such as heat in electronic circuits, called thermal noise) or artificial sources (interference from other devices, for example a microwave near a Wi-Fi router). We have seen above that noise limits the capacity of a channel by making signals more difficult to decode.

Noise, in a traditional system, can be managed by amplifiers and analog components. In SDR, noise is often reduced by digital algorithms that dynamically adapt signal processing. This allows maintaining optimal signal quality in noisy environments.

#### Baseband filtering

Filtering a baseband signal is essential for several reasons in communication systems. It mainly helps reduce inter-symbol interference. This filtering also limits the signal bandwidth, which helps avoid spectral waste and comply with frequency regulations imposed by authorities.

Thus, baseband filtering is an essential concept in the field of software-defined radio. Among the most commonly used types of filters we find:

- Gaussian filter
- Raised cosine filter
- Matched filters

Each of these filters presents specific advantages and challenges, depending on the application context. While in traditional radio systems, filtering is performed with analog components, in SDR, filters are implemented digitally. This allows increased flexibility, as it is possible to modify filter characteristics simply by programming, without needing to change hardware.

#### Conclusion

Software-defined radio (SDR) relies on fundamental concepts such as baseband signal, modulation, noise management, and filtering, which enable the design of flexible and efficient communication systems. Shannon's information theory provides a theoretical framework for optimizing communication channel capacity, while advances in digital signal processing have allowed SDR to revolutionize telecommunications. By combining techniques such as digital modulation and baseband filtering, SDR offers unprecedented adaptability, making communication systems more robust against interference and constraints of modern environments.
