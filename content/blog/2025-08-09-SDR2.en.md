In this section, we will explore the fundamental concepts of radio, essential for understanding how Software-Defined Radios (SDR) work. We will cover key notions such as baseband, modulation, and channel capacity. These concepts will form the foundation for understanding the technical challenges that SDRs address.

#### Baseband

A baseband signal refers to the original signal as produced by the source, before any transformation for transmission. For example, a digital audio file is a baseband signal: its spectrum is centered around zero frequency and extends to a maximum frequency related to its content (a few kHz for voice).
Modulation

To transmit this signal over a physical medium (cable, fiber, radio waves), it is often necessary to modulate it. Modulation consists of “carrying” the information on a higher-frequency carrier wave in order to:

- Adapt the signal to the transmission channel,
- Enable simultaneous transmission of multiple signals (multiplexing),
- Reduce interference in certain media.

There are several types of modulation:

- Amplitude Modulation (AM) – the carrier’s amplitude varies according to the signal being transmitted.
- Frequency Modulation (FM) – the carrier’s frequency varies with the signal.
- Phase Modulation (PM) – the carrier’s phase changes to encode the information.

#### Shannon and Channel Capacity

In 1948, Claude Elwood Shannon modeled, for the first time, a communication channel in his paper A Mathematical Theory of Communication. This model describes all the components involved in transmitting a signal, from the information source to its destination.

Shannon was a pioneer in information theory. He established a fundamental theorem that defines the capacity of a channel.

The formula modeling this is:

$$ C = BW \log_{2}{(1+\frac{S}{N})} $$

- C – Channel capacity (bits/s)
- BW – Bandwidth (Hz)
- S/N – Signal-to-noise ratio (dimensionless, linear scale)

Shannon’s formula calculates the maximum capacity of a channel—that is, the maximum amount of information it can transmit per second. The capacity depends on two key factors:

- The bandwidth
- The signal-to-noise ratio

In practice, this formula helps us understand how to maximize information transfer in systems such as wireless networks.

** Example ** – Theoretical Capacity vs. Actual Throughput (Wi-Fi case)

Let’s consider a Wi-Fi network:

* Available bandwidth: B=20 MHzB=20MHz
* Measured signal-to-noise ratio: S/N=25 dBS/N=25dB

1) Convert the S/N ratio:

$$ \frac{S}{N} = 10^{\frac{25}{10}} = 10^{2.5} \approx 316,23 $$

2) Apply Shannon’s formula:

$$ C = BW \log_{2}{(1+\frac{S}{N})} $$
$$ C = 20 \cdot 10^{6} \log_{2}{(1+316,23)} $$
$$ C \approx  20 \cdot 10^{6} \cdot 8,31 \approx 166.2 Mbit/s $$

The theoretical capacity is about 166 Mb/s, whereas the actual throughput is often between 80 Mb/s and 120 Mb/s. This difference is due to losses, interference, protocol overhead, and collisions. In practice, the real throughput is always lower than the maximum channel capacity.

#### Noise

Noise is any unwanted disturbance that corrupts a radio signal. It can come from natural sources (such as thermal noise from heat in electronic circuits) or from artificial sources (such as interference from other devices—for example, a microwave near a Wi-Fi router).

As we saw earlier, noise limits a channel’s capacity by making signals harder to decode.

In traditional systems, noise can be mitigated with amplifiers and analog components. In SDR systems, noise is often reduced using digital algorithms that dynamically adapt signal processing. This makes it possible to maintain optimal signal quality in noisy environments.

#### Baseband Filtering

Filtering a baseband signal is essential in communication systems for several reasons. Primarily, it helps reduce inter-symbol interference. Filtering also limits the signal’s bandwidth, preventing spectral waste and ensuring compliance with frequency regulations.

Baseband filtering is therefore a key concept in software-defined radio. Common filter types include:

- Gaussian filters
- Raised cosine filters
- Matched filters

Each filter type offers specific advantages and challenges depending on the application context.

Whereas in traditional radio systems filtering is done using analog components, in SDR it is implemented digitally. This offers greater flexibility, since the filter’s characteristics can be changed simply through software programming, without the need to modify hardware.
