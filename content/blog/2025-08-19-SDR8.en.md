#### Fundamental principles of digital modulation

Digital modulation consists of modifying the characteristics of a sinusoidal carrier (amplitude, frequency or phase) according to the binary data to be transmitted. Unlike analog modulation where the modulating signal is continuous, digital modulation processes discrete bit sequences, generally organized into symbols.

The process begins with the mapping of information bits onto symbols, each symbol being able to represent one or several bits depending on the modulation complexity. These symbols are then used to modify the carrier according to the chosen technique. Spectral efficiency, measured in bits per second per hertz, constitutes a crucial parameter determining the amount of information transmittable in a given frequency band.

#### Amplitude modulations

Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK) represents the simplest form of digital modulation. In binary ASK, two distinct amplitudes correspond to bits 0 and 1. Generally, the absence of carrier (zero amplitude) represents bit 0, while a given amplitude represents bit 1. This technique, although simple to implement, presents significant sensitivity to noise and channel amplitude variations.

Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) combines amplitude and phase modulations, allowing high spectral efficiencies to be achieved. In QAM, symbols are positioned in the complex plane according to a regular geometric constellation. QAM-16, QAM-64 or QAM-256 modulations transmit respectively 4, 6 or 8 bits per symbol, thus multiplying the data rate without widening the bandwidth.

#### Frequency modulations

Frequency Shift Keying (FSK) uses different carrier frequencies to represent binary symbols. In binary FSK, two distinct frequencies \( f_1 \) and \( f_2 \) correspond to bits 0 and 1. The spacing between these frequencies, called frequency deviation, directly influences performance in terms of bit error rate and spectral occupancy.

Continuous Phase FSK (CPFSK) improves spectral efficiency by maintaining phase continuity between symbol transitions. Gaussian FSK (GFSK), used notably in GSM systems, applies Gaussian filtering to binary data before FSK modulation, thus reducing spectral side lobes and interference between adjacent channels.

#### Phase modulations

Phase Shift Keying (PSK) modifies the carrier phase according to the data to be transmitted. In binary PSK (BPSK), two opposite phases (0° and 180°) represent bits 0 and 1. This technique offers excellent noise robustness, particularly suited to applications requiring high reliability.

Quaternary PSK (QPSK) uses four equally spaced phases (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°) allowing transmission of 2 bits per symbol. Each QPSK symbol corresponds to a bit pair, thus doubling spectral efficiency compared to BPSK without increasing the necessary bandwidth.

Higher-order PSK modulations (8-PSK, 16-PSK) increase the number of bits per symbol but at the cost of increased sensitivity to noise and phase distortions. The Euclidean distance between constellation points decreases with increasing modulation order, degrading performance in the presence of noise.

#### Demodulation process

Digital demodulation recovers the original binary data from the received modulated signal. This complex process comprises several critical steps: carrier synchronization, symbol synchronization, channel equalization, and decision on received symbols.

Coherent demodulation requires reconstruction of the carrier in phase with that of the transmitter. Phase-locked loops (PLL) and carrier recovery algorithms (Costas loop) enable this synchronization. The precision of this step directly influences demodulation performance, particularly for phase modulations.

Differential demodulation avoids the need for absolute carrier recovery by encoding information in transitions between consecutive symbols. Although less sensitive to phase errors, this technique generally presents poorer performance in terms of bit error rate.

#### Conclusion

This article has explored digital modulation and demodulation techniques, fundamental elements of modern telecommunications. These methods enable efficient transmission of binary data by optimizing radio spectrum utilization. The different approaches (ASK, FSK, PSK, QAM) offer solutions adapted to the specific constraints of each application, balancing robustness, spectral efficiency and implementation complexity for the evolution of communication systems.
