Software-defined radio (SDR) owes its flexibility to the fact that most modulation, demodulation, and filtering operations are performed digitally. But to efficiently handle an RF signal in the digital domain, a representation that preserves the full amplitude and phase information is required. This is precisely the role of the IQ architecture: to provide the processor with a complex signal composed of an In-phase (I) component and a Quadrature (Q) component, perfectly synchronized and shifted by 90°. This representation, inherited from complex signal theory, has become indispensable in SDR.

#### Origin and Principle of the IQ Representation

A pure RF signal can be described by a sinusoid:

$$ s(t) = A \cos(\omega t + \phi)$$

In practice, a modulated signal occupies a certain bandwidth around the carrier frequency ωω and contains variations in amplitude and phase. To process it efficiently, it is preferable to represent it as a combination of two orthogonal components:

$$ s(t) = I (t) \cos(\omega t) - Q(t) \sin (\omega t)$$

where I(t)I(t) and Q(t)Q(t) are two baseband signals carrying, respectively, the real and imaginary parts of a complex version of the signal. In this notation, the signal becomes:

$$ s_{c} = I(t) + j Q(t)$$

This approach makes it possible to translate modulated signals into a complex baseband representation, where operations such as frequency shifting, filtering, and demodulation become mathematically straightforward.

#### Hardware Implementation of the IQ Architecture

In reception, the generation of the IQ stream relies on a quadrature mixer. The incoming RF signal is first split into two identical paths. The first is multiplied by a cosine local oscillator (LO) signal. The second is multiplied by a sine LO signal, obtained by introducing an exact 90° phase shift to the LO. The mixing products contain both the sum and the difference of the input and LO frequencies; a low-pass filter retains only the intermediate frequency component near zero. Each filtered path is then digitized by an independent analog-to-digital converter (ADC), producing two parallel streams: I[n]I[n] and Q[n]Q[n].

In transmission, the process is symmetrical: the digital I and Q streams generated by the processor are fed into digital-to-analog converters (DACs), then recombined by a quadrature mixer to reconstruct a real RF signal.

#### Advantages of the IQ Representation

Using a complex baseband signal allows both the positive and negative components of the spectrum to be represented unambiguously, effectively doubling the usable bandwidth compared to a real signal sampled at the same rate. Complex modulations such as QPSK, QAM, OFDM, or any vector modulation are naturally expressed in this formalism. Furthermore, frequency shifts can be implemented simply through complex multiplication, eliminating the need for additional analog operations and making SDR systems inherently more flexible.

#### Limitations and Real-World Imperfections

In practice, generating perfectly matched I and Q channels is impossible. Small amplitude imbalances between the two paths, phase errors relative to the ideal 90°, or nonlinearities introduce imperfect image rejection. These imperfections manifest as an unwanted mirror signal symmetric with respect to the center frequency. For example, a 2° phase error or a 0.5 dB amplitude imbalance can limit image rejection to less than 40 dB. In modern SDR systems, however, these defects can be mitigated through digital calibration algorithms, often executed automatically at startup or continuously during operation.

#### Role of IQ in the SDR Signal Chain

In a direct-sampling SDR architecture, the ADC output may already provide an IQ stream if the conversion stage integrates IQ generation internally. In heterodyne architectures, IQ generation takes place after one or more intermediate frequency shifts but before purely digital processing. In all cases, it is this IQ stream that is delivered to the FPGA, DSP, or host processor for analysis, filtering, demodulation, or retransmission.

#### Conclusion

The IQ architecture is far more than a simple representation convention—it is a prerequisite for fully leveraging the power of digital processing in SDR. By simultaneously providing both the amplitude and phase of the signal, it enables direct manipulation of complex spectra, implementation of advanced modulation schemes, and digital correction of hardware imperfections. Mastery of this architecture, both in theory and in practice, is an essential skill for any engineer involved in the design or operation of modern SDR systems.
