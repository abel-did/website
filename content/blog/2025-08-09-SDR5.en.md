Software-defined radio (SDR) owes its flexibility to the fact that most modulation, demodulation and filtering operations are performed digitally. But to efficiently manipulate an RF signal in the digital domain, one must have a representation that preserves the entirety of amplitude and phase information. This is precisely the role of IQ architecture: providing the processor with a complex signal, composed of an "In phase" (I) part and a "Quadrature" (Q) part, perfectly synchronized and phase-shifted by 90°. This representation, inherited from complex signal theory, has become essential in SDR.

#### Origin and principle of IQ representation

A pure RF signal can be described by a sinusoid:

$$ s(t) = A \cos(\omega t + \phi)$$

In practice, a modulated signal occupies a certain band around the carrier ω and contains amplitude and phase variations. To process it efficiently, it is preferable to represent it as a combination of two orthogonal components:

$$ s(t) = I (t) \cos(\omega t) - Q(t) \sin (\omega t)$$

where I(t) and Q(t) are two low-frequency signals carrying respectively the real and imaginary parts of a complex version of the signal. In this notation, the signal becomes:

$$ s_{c} = I(t) + j Q(t)$$

This approach allows transposing modulated signals to complex baseband, where processing operations (frequency shifting, filtering, demodulation) are mathematically simple.

#### Hardware implementation of IQ architecture

In reception, IQ stream generation relies on a quadrature mixer. The input RF signal is first divided into two identical paths. The first is multiplied by a local cosine wave, generated by the local oscillator (LO). The second is multiplied by a local sine wave, obtained by introducing a precise 90° phase shift on the LO. The mixing products contain the sum and difference of input and LO frequencies; low-pass filtering preserves only the intermediate frequency component close to zero. Each filtered path is then digitized by an independent analog-to-digital converter, thus providing two parallel streams: I[n] and Q[n].

In transmission, the path is symmetrical: the digital I and Q streams generated by the processor pass through digital-to-analog converters (DAC), then are recombined by a quadrature mixer to reconstruct a real RF signal.

#### Advantages of IQ representation

Using a complex baseband signal allows simultaneous representation of positive and negative spectral components without ambiguity, which doubles the useful bandwidth compared to a real signal sampled at the same frequency. Complex modulations like QPSK, QAM, OFDM or any vector modulation are naturally expressed in this formalism. Furthermore, frequency shifts translate simply to complex multiplication, which avoids additional analog operations and makes SDR intrinsically more flexible.

#### Limitations and real imperfections

In practice, generating perfect I and Q channels is impossible. Small amplitude imbalances between the two channels, phase errors relative to the ideal 90°, or nonlinearities introduce imperfect image rejection. These defects manifest as the presence of an unwanted mirror signal symmetrical relative to the center frequency. For example, a phase error of 2° or an imbalance of 0.5 dB can limit image rejection to less than 40 dB. In modern SDR, these defects can however be mitigated by digital calibration algorithms, often executed automatically at startup or continuously.

#### Place of IQ in the SDR chain

In a direct sampling SDR architecture, the ADC output can already provide an IQ stream if the conversion architecture integrates it internally. In heterodyne architectures, IQ generation occurs after one or several intermediate frequency changes, but before purely digital processing. In all cases, it is this IQ stream that is transmitted to the FPGA, DSP or host processor to be analyzed, filtered, demodulated or retransmitted.

#### Conclusion

IQ architecture is much more than a simple representation convention: it is a necessary condition to fully exploit the power of digital processing in SDR. By simultaneously providing signal amplitude and phase, it makes possible direct manipulation of complex spectra, implementation of sophisticated modulations and digital correction of hardware imperfections. Mastery of this architecture, both theoretically and practically, is an essential prerequisite for any engineer working on the design or operation of modern SDR systems.
