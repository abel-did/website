Welcome to this series of articles dedicated to Software-Defined Radio (SDR).
SDR is a technology that is transforming the way radio systems are designed and deployed. Traditionally, radios were built with dedicated hardware components for each signal-processing function (amplification, modulation, demodulation, etc.). By contrast, in an SDR, much of this processing is carried out by software, enabling greater flexibility, reusability, and adaptability.

### Origins and Evolution of SDR

#### Early Explorations (1960s–1970s)

The concept of software-defined radio dates back to the 1960s–1970s, but it was only with the rise of computers and digital technologies that the idea truly began to take shape. The notion of a “programmable” or adaptable radio emerged when researchers started exploring ways to process signals using software.

At that time, most radios were primarily analog. The need for more flexible radios, capable of handling different types of signals, was evident in both telecommunications and the military, where systems needed to adapt to various modulation schemes and frequency ranges.

#### The Birth of Modern SDR (1980s–1990s)

In the 1980s, advances in Digital Signal Processors (DSPs) enabled more sophisticated digital signal processing, laying the groundwork for modern SDR. Research intensified around digital modulation, with scientists exploring the possibility of replacing certain hardware components with software—especially for radio signal processing.

The term Software-Defined Radio was introduced in 1984 by Joseph Mitola. In 1992, he published a seminal paper describing SDR as a radio that uses software to perform signal-processing functions previously handled by hardware components.

#### Commercial and Military Growth (2000s)

In the 2000s, SDR increasingly became a reality in both commercial and military applications. The military, in particular, recognized SDR as a way to reduce the complexity and costs of communication systems. It allowed rapid adaptation of communication frequencies and protocols without replacing hardware—especially valuable in combat environments where flexibility is critical.

In 2000, companies such as National Instruments and Ettus Research began developing accessible SDR platforms, such as the NI-USRP (Universal Software Radio Peripheral) and FlexRadio Systems, facilitating SDR implementations across diverse contexts—from wireless networks to academic research and radio signal monitoring.

#### The Era of Democratization and Open Source (2010s and Beyond)

The 2010s marked the true democratization of SDR. Open-source platforms such as GNU Radio, SDR# (SDRSharp), and HDSDR made SDR technology accessible to a wide audience, from hobbyists to researchers. These tools allowed users to build their own SDR setups at relatively low cost, transforming how scientists, engineers, and enthusiasts approach radio technology.

The emergence of devices like the Raspberry Pi and affordable hardware components such as the RTL-SDR made entry into the SDR world highly cost-effective, opening the door to a new generation of amateur radio operators and innovators.

### Conclusion

The history of software-defined radio is defined by a series of breakthroughs in digital signal processing. From early research in the 1960s to the open-source democratization of the 2010s, SDR has radically transformed the way communication systems are designed and used. Thanks to pioneers like Joseph Mitola and its initial military implementations, SDR has now become a cornerstone of modern telecommunications.
