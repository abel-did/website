Welcome to this series of articles dedicated to Software-Defined Radio (SDR). SDR is a technology that transforms how radio systems are designed and deployed. Traditionally, radios were built with specific hardware components for each signal processing function (amplification, modulation, demodulation, etc.). In contrast, in an SDR, much of this processing is performed using software, enabling greater flexibility, reusability, and adaptability.

#### Origins and Evolution of SDR

#### Early Explorations (1960s - 1970s)

The concept of software-defined radio dates back to the 1960s-1970s, but it wasn't until the rise of computers and digital technologies that the idea truly began to take shape. The idea of "programmable radio" or adaptable radio emerged when researchers began exploring ways to process signals using software.

At that time, early radios were primarily analog. The need for more flexible radios capable of processing different types of signals became evident in telecommunications and military domains, which sought systems capable of adapting to different types of modulation and frequencies.

#### The Birth of Modern SDR (1980s-1990s)

In the 1980s, the advancement of digital signal processors (DSP) enabled more advanced digital signal processing, laying the foundation for modern SDR. Research intensified around digital modulation, with researchers beginning to explore the possibility of replacing specific hardware components with software, particularly for radio signal processing.

The term "Software Defined Radio" was introduced in 1984 by Joseph Mitola. In 1992, he published his seminal article describing SDR as a radio that uses software to perform signal processing functions that were previously done by hardware components.

#### Commercial and Military Rise (2000s)

In the 2000s, SDR increasingly became a reality in commercial and military domains. The military, in particular, saw SDR as a means to reduce complexity and costs of communication systems. It allowed for rapid adaptation of communication frequencies and protocols without having to replace hardware, especially in warfare environments where flexibility is crucial.

![SDR](/images/2025-08-09-sdr1/en/sdr_mil.jpg "Software Defined Radio enables enhanced military communications - Military Embedded Systems")

In 2000, companies like National Instruments and Ettus Research began developing accessible SDR platforms, such as the NI-USRP (Universal Software Radio Peripheral) and FlexRadio systems, which facilitated the transition to SDR implementations in contexts as varied as wireless networks, research, and radio signal monitoring.

#### The Era of Democratization and Open-Source (2010 and Beyond)

The 2010s marked a true democratization of SDR. Open-source platforms like GNU Radio, SDR# (SDRSharp), or HDSDR made SDR technologies accessible to a broad audience, from hobbyists to researchers. These software platforms and tools allowed users to build their own SDR equipment at relatively low cost, transforming how researchers, engineers, and hobbyists use radio technologies.

The emergence of Raspberry Pi and affordable hardware components like RTL-SDR enabled entry into the SDR world at very competitive prices, thus opening the door to a new generation of radio amateurs and innovators.

#### In Conclusion

The history of software-defined radio is marked by a series of technological breakthroughs in digital signal processing. From the first research in the 1960s to democratization via open-source platforms in the 2010s, SDR has radically changed how communication systems are designed and used. Thanks to pioneers like Joseph Mitola and early implementations in the military domain, SDR is now a key element of modern telecommunications.
