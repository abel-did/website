In this article, we’ll explore one of the most widely used architectures in radio signal processing: the heterodyne.

#### Basic Components of an RF Chain

An RF (radio frequency) chain is made up of components that work together to receive, amplify, filter, convert, and transmit a radio signal. Everything starts with the antenna, which captures or emits electromagnetic waves. The received signal then passes through an RF filter, which selects only the desired frequency band and removes unwanted signals.

A low-noise amplifier (LNA) then boosts this weak signal while preserving its quality. The mixer comes next, combining the RF signal with a reference signal provided by a local oscillator, allowing the signal’s frequency to be shifted.

On the transmit side, a power amplifier boosts the signal before sending it to the antenna. Finally, when signals need to be processed digitally, analog-to-digital and digital-to-analog converters handle the transition between the analog and digital domains.

#### The Principle of the Heterodyne in Radio

The heterodyne method, invented in the early 20th century, simplifies radio signal processing. Instead of working directly with the original signal frequency—often very high—it is mixed with a signal generated by a local oscillator to produce a lower intermediate frequency (IF).

This conversion does not alter the information contained in the signal, but shifts it to a frequency that is easier to amplify and filter.

For example, if a receiver picks up a signal at 100 MHz and the local oscillator generates a 90 MHz signal, the mixer will produce, among other things, a 10 MHz signal. This intermediate frequency, much lower than the original RF, allows the use of simpler and more efficient circuits.

#### Specific Components in a Heterodyne Architecture

In a heterodyne architecture, the signal follows a specific path:
After being captured by the antenna and cleaned by the RF filter, the signal is amplified by the LNA. It is then sent to the mixer, which uses the local oscillator’s frequency to convert it into an intermediate frequency.

This IF is then passed through a dedicated IF filter, which precisely isolates the desired band, and then through an IF amplifier to boost its level. Finally, a demodulator extracts the useful information.

#### The Principle of the Heterodyne Mixer

The mixer is a key component in a heterodyne chain. It performs frequency conversion—in electronics, “mixing” means multiplying two signals rather than blending them.

If the RF signal captured by the antenna is:

$$ s_{RF} (t) = A_{RF} \cdot \cos(2\pi f_{RF}t) $$

and the local oscillator produces:

$$ s_{LO} (t) = A_{LO} \cdot \cos(2\pi f_{LO}t) $$

then the mixer performs the multiplication:

$$ s_{out} (t) = s_{RF} (t) \cdot s_{LO} (t) $$

Using the trigonometric identity:

$$ \cos(A) \cdot \cos(B) = \frac{1}{2} [\cos(A+B) + \cos(A-B)] $$

we get:

$$ s_{out} = \frac{A_{RF} A_{LO}}{2} [cos (2\pi (f_{RF}+f_{LO})t) + cos (2\pi (f_{RF}-f_{LO})t)] $$

This means the mixer produces two new frequencies:

- One at the sum of the input frequencies
- One at the difference

In practice, an RF filter selects the difference component, called the intermediate frequency (IF):

$$ f_{FI} = |f_{RF} - f_{LO}| $$

This IF is then amplified and processed, since it is much easier to handle than the direct RF signal.

#### Advantages of the Heterodyne Architecture

This architecture offers several advantages. First, it provides excellent selectivity thanks to precise filtering at the intermediate frequency, making it easy to choose the desired station or channel while eliminating unwanted signals. It also improves receiver sensitivity, enabling the reception of very weak signals. In addition, it is highly flexible: simply changing the local oscillator frequency allows coverage of different radio bands without altering the entire circuit. Finally, it offers better operational stability, as the circuits operate at a lower frequency than the original RF, reducing noise and distortion.
