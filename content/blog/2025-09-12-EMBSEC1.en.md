Refrigerators now send notifications to smartphones. Thermostats adapt to occupants’ habits. Smartwatches monitor heart rates in real time. These devices form the Internet of Things (IoT). Each device functions as a small connected computer.
The IoT revolution is transforming daily life while introducing new security risks. Every sensor represents a potential entry point for attackers. Securing these billions of devices has become essential. This article series explores the challenges of IoT security.

#### European Regulatory Framework: RED and EN Standards for IoT

##### The RED Directive Strengthens IoT Security

The European Union enhances the cybersecurity of connected devices through the RED Directive 2014/53/EU, which redefines market requirements. Article 3.3(d) represents a key milestone: every IoT device must incorporate protections against fraud and ensure the security of personal data. For example, a temperature sensor must demonstrate compliance to be marketed in Europe.
This reform addresses a major issue. The rapid growth of IoT has often prioritized low costs over security, resulting in weak passwords, unencrypted communications, and a lack of updates. The RED Directive puts an end to these risky practices.

##### EN Standards Guide Secure Implementation

European EN standards translate legal obligations into practical technical specifications to guide the implementation of secure solutions.
The EN 303 645 standard focuses on the security of consumer IoT devices. It defines thirteen fundamental requirements, such as prohibiting universal default passwords, mandating secure updates, reducing exposed services, and requiring encryption for all communications. For instance, a surveillance camera must have a unique password per device, automatic updates, and minimal open network ports.
The EN 303 417 standard addresses modern radio communications, defining requirements for 5G and WiFi 6.
The EN 62443 standard applies to industrial IoT, addressing aspects such as network segmentation and centralized identity management.

#### IoT Secure Development Process

Secure development of IoT devices relies on a methodology tailored to their specific constraints. These devices, often limited in energy and computing resources, must meet high security standards while operating in large-scale deployments where maintenance can be complex.
This methodology is divided into six main phases. Each phase considers IoT-specific characteristics, balancing security, performance, and hardware constraints to ensure reliable and efficient solutions.

##### Identification of Critical IoT Assets

The first step involves identifying all assets that require protection, going beyond a simple technical inventory.
Hardware components include microcontrollers that process sensitive data, sensors that collect personal information, actuators that execute critical commands, and memory that stores programs and cryptographic keys.
Data is particularly sensitive. Telemetry data can reveal user behaviors, connection metadata exposes network architecture, cryptographic keys secure the ecosystem, and authentication certificates validate legitimate identities.
Intangible assets play a significant role. Protecting user privacy is a priority, service availability impacts user experience, reliability strengthens brand reputation, and regulatory compliance prevents costly penalties.
Criticality assessment accounts for IoT-specific factors. For example, a temperature sensor in a greenhouse has lower criticality than a connected medical device. This detailed analysis enables prioritization of protection measures.

##### Understanding IoT Threats

Due to their unique characteristics, IoT devices are vulnerable to sophisticated and varied threats.
IoT botnets are among the most visible threats. Attackers compromise millions of connected devices, turning them into zombies to launch massive DDoS attacks. For example, the 2016 Mirai attack infected 600,000 devices, generating traffic of 1 Tbps.
IoT espionage is particularly concerning. Voice assistants continuously record, cameras monitor behaviors, and motion sensors track movements with precision. A compromised device allows attackers to access sensitive data, enabling intrusive surveillance.
Physical attacks exploit the accessibility of IoT devices, often deployed without sufficient physical protection, which expands the attack surface.
Denial-of-service attacks target energy constraints. By forcing battery-powered devices to perform intensive computations, attackers deplete their energy, rendering the devices inoperable.

##### Implementation of IoT Security Requirements

Translating needs into technical requirements for IoT demands an approach tailored to its constraints. Balancing energy performance, production costs, and security involves complex trade-offs.
Security requirements must be precise. For example, strong authentication may translate to mutual authentication based on ECC-256 certificates, renewed automatically every 30 days, with a fallback mechanism for connectivity loss.
The security architecture must account for energy constraints. Each cryptographic operation consumes energy, necessitating a balance between security and efficiency. Dedicated cryptographic chips and optimized communication protocols reduce this consumption.
Scalability poses a major challenge. The infrastructure must manage cryptographic keys and authentication certificates for thousands of connected devices while remaining reliable.
Hardware constraints require rigorous optimization. For instance, an ARM Cortex-M0+ microcontroller with 32 KB of flash memory and 4 KB of RAM demands meticulous code optimization.
Modern IoT frameworks, such as Zephyr, FreeRTOS, or Mbed OS, facilitate secure implementation by integrating optimized security mechanisms and proven protocols, reducing development complexity.
Cryptographic key management is a critical technical challenge. Secure elements, like Atmel’s ATECC608 chips, provide hardware-based protection against physical attacks, though their integration slightly increases costs.

##### Validation and Maintenance of IoT Security

IoT security testing requires tailored approaches, combining standard software checks with analyses of vulnerabilities related to energy and environmental constraints.
Energy consumption tests under attack reveal specific vulnerabilities. An attacker can force a sensor to perform intensive computations, rapidly draining its battery and rendering the device unusable.
Network robustness tests are critical. IoT devices often operate with unstable connections and limited bandwidth. Authentication mechanisms must remain operational after hours of disconnection.
Physical security tests are essential. IoT devices, often physically accessible, require intrusion tests to verify whether attackers can extract cryptographic keys by tampering with the hardware.
Scalability tests simulate real-world conditions. The security infrastructure must manage thousands of connected devices simultaneously in realistic scenarios.
Security maintenance is complex, as devices may remain deployed for decades in hard-to-access environments.
Vulnerability monitoring requires an automated approach to correlate newly published vulnerabilities with affected devices, enabling rapid risk identification.
Large-scale secure updates are a complex challenge. Deploying a patch across 100,000 geographically dispersed smart meters requires sophisticated and secure orchestration.

#### Conclusion

The Internet of Things integrates computing into daily life, offering significant benefits but also exposing risks if vulnerabilities proliferate. Each connected sensor is a critical link in our collective security.
The European regulatory framework is progressing positively. The RED Directive transforms recommendations into strict legal obligations, marking a turning point for the IoT industry. Security is becoming a competitive advantage in the market.
A secure development methodology tailored to IoT constraints provides a proven framework. Although the initial investment in security may seem high, the cost of inaction grows exponentially with deployment scale.
Future articles in this series will explore practical aspects of IoT security, detailing the selection of cryptographic components, network architecture, and secure update mechanisms.
The future of IoT depends on current technical choices. Each designed device either strengthens a secure IoT ecosystem or contributes to an uncontrollable attack surface. Developers’ expertise plays a pivotal role in building an inherently secure IoT.
